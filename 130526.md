For a travel site focused on "Curated Expeditions," GPX data is your most valuable asset. It transforms a simple list of places into a **navigable journey**.

Here are the best resources for GPX data and a content strategy to turn raw coordinates into high-end travel products.

---

### **1\. GPX Data Resources (APIs & Databases)**

#### **The "Outdoors & Adventure" Giants (Hiking/Alps/Deserts)**

* **Komoot Partner API:** The gold standard for hiking and cycling in Europe. Their data includes "Surface" (gravel vs. asphalt) and "Difficulty" metrics.  
* **Outdooractive API:** This is the primary data source for the Alps and many European tourism boards. It provides high-quality, professional-grade GPX tracks with high-resolution topo maps.  
* **Wikiloc API:** One of the world’s largest databases of user-generated GPX files. Excellent for finding "niche" trails in deserts or rainforests that aren't on official maps.  
* **AllTrails (Scraping/Export):** They don’t have a public API for small developers, but they are the most comprehensive source for North America. Many developers use **Apify** to scrape specific trail URLs to get the GPX/JSON data.  
* **Strava Route API:** Great if your expeditions are performance-based (e.g., "The Most Challenging Alpine Climbs").

#### **Road Trips & Scenic Drives**

* **Roadtrippers API:** Specializes in the "Waypoints" between destinations. It’s perfect for generating the "curated" part of a road trip (world's largest ball of twine, etc.).  
* **Kurviger API:** Originally for motorcyclists, it’s arguably the best API for finding "curvy" and "scenic" roads rather than the fastest highway.  
* **OpenStreetMap (OSM) Relations:** You can use **Overpass Turbo** to query "Relations." For example: `relation["route"="tourist"]`. This will give you the GPX coordinates for established routes like the *Route 66* or the *Romantic Road* in Germany.

#### **City & Historical Routes**

* **GPSmyCity:** They have thousands of self-guided walking tours. While they don't have a public API, their data is a map for how to structure a city-based "expedition."  
* **VoiceMap API:** They provide audio-tour GPX tracks. This is perfect for your "Historical" or "Movie" routes because the GPX data is already synced to a narrative.  
* **UNESCO World Heritage Data:** You can download the spatial data (KML/GPX) of all UNESCO sites to build "Historical Wonders" expeditions.

#### **Movie & Thematic Routes**

* **Movie-Locations.com:** The most comprehensive database for filming locations. You would likely need to scrape the coordinates and use a routing engine (like **Mapbox Directions API**) to "snap" those points into a GPX path.  
* **Set-Jetters:** An app dedicated to movie scenes. Exploring their data can help you find the exact "photo-op" coordinates for a movie-themed route.

### **2\. Brainstorming: Content Strategy for GPX Data**

Having a GPX file is just the beginning. To make it a "Curated Expedition," you need to **wrap the data in a story.**

#### **A. Data Augmentation (The "Rich" Route)**

Don't just show a line on a map. Use APIs to add layers to the GPX data:

* **Elevation Profiles:** Use the **Google Elevation API** or **Open-Elevation** to show the "effort" required for the route.  
* **Weather-Along-Route:** Use the **OpenWeatherMap API** to show the user the weather at *every hour* of their journey based on their travel speed.  
* **POIs Snap-to-Route:** Use a "Buffer" logic. If a user is on a GPX track, show them "Curated Shops" that are only within 500 meters of that specific path.

#### **B. Thematic "Wrappers"**

Create "Campaigns" based on the GPX routes:

* **The "Cine-Trek":** A GPX route through Northern Ireland that stops at every *Game of Thrones* filming location, with "Behind the scenes" photos appearing as waypoints.  
* **The "Slow Food Road Trip":** A driving GPX through Tuscany where every waypoint is a farm that allows on-site tasting.  
* **The "Ghost Path":** A historical walking GPX of London or New Orleans that only activates waypoints after sunset (using a simple time-of-day logic in your app).

#### **C. Interactive Utility (The "Utility" Strategy)**

* **"Send to GPS":** This is a killer feature. Provide an easy button to send the GPX directly to a user’s **Garmin**, **Wahoo**, or **Google Maps**.  
* **The "Detour" Score:** Use your logic to tell users: *"If you take this 10-minute GPX detour, you'll see a hidden waterfall."* This makes the curation feel active, not passive.  
* **Difficulty/Safety Ratings:** Use data to warn users if a GPX route is currently dangerous (e.g., "Snowed in" for Alps routes or "High Heat" for Desert routes) by cross-referencing recent social media mentions or weather data.

#### **D. Community & Social Proof**

* **FKT (Fastest Known Time):** If your site is for hikers/runners, integrate a leaderboard for your curated GPX routes.  
* **"I Was Here" Waypoints:** Allow users to upload photos *tied to a specific coordinate on your GPX path*. This creates a living, breathing visual guide for the next traveler.

### **Content Strategy for GPX Data**

To differentiate your site, you must transform raw GPX lines into **dynamic stories**:

* **The "Contextual Waypoint" Layer:** Instead of just a path, use **Wikipedia's API (MediaWiki)** to automatically pull history for every waypoint on a historical route.  
* **"Movie Scene" Overlay:** For movie routes, show a thumbnail of the actual film scene when the user’s GPS coordinate matches the filming site.  
* **Safety & Status Integration:** Cross-reference GPX routes with **OpenWeather** or **NASA FIRMS** (for forest fires) to show real-time "Route Health" scores.  
* **Difficulty Normalization:** Use **Google Elevation API** to create a standard "Strenuousness" rating. A "3/10" in the Alps might be a "9/10" for a city walker; your site should normalize these based on user profile.  
* **The "Detour" Engine:** Allow users to "Add a Niche Stop." If they are on a Road Trip GPX, use the **Foursquare API** to suggest one "Hidden Gem" shop that is within a 5-minute drive of their current path.

To make your app truly "curated," you shouldn't treat GPX files as just lines on a map. Think of them as the **programmable backbone** of a story.

A GPX file is essentially an XML document. Its primary power lies in three standard tags: `<wpt>` (Waypoints/POIs), `<rte>` (Routes/Planned path), and `<trk>` (Tracks/Actual recorded path).

Here is how to maximize those capacities for your travel planner:

### **1\. The "Narrative" Capacity: Triggered Content**

Standard GPX files allow for a `<desc>` (description) and `<link>` tag within any waypoint. You can use this to turn a coordinate into a **storytelling trigger**:

* **Audio Guides:** When a user's GPS coordinate falls within a 20-meter radius of a `<wpt>`, your app can auto-play an audio clip. This is perfect for "Historical" or "Movie" routes.  
* **Augmented Reality (AR):** Use waypoints to anchor AR "ghosts." On a historical route, a waypoint at a ruin could trigger a 3D overlay on the user's phone showing the building as it was in 1850\.  
* **"Scene" Mode:** For movie routes, when a user hits a waypoint, display a "Then vs. Now" slider using the `<link>` tag to pull images of the film scene vs. the current location.

### **2\. The "Logistics" Capacity: Smart Waypoints**

Waypoints aren't just for sightseeing; they are for survival and comfort.

* **Opening Hours Integration:** Cross-reference your GPX waypoints with the Google Places API. If a user is on a "Local Shops" route, your app can warn them: *"You're 10 mins from the pottery shop, but it closes in 15 mins. Speed up\!"*  
* **Supply Points:** For "Extreme" or "Alps" routes, use a specific `<type>` tag in your GPX (e.g., `<type>water_refill</type>`) to create a "Safety Dashboard" that tells the user how many kilometers until the next reliable water source.

### **3\. The "Performance" Capacity: Elevation & Effort**

GPX files store `<ele>` (elevation) data for every single point.

* **Dynamic Difficulty:** Don't just label a hike "Hard." Calculate the **Gradient** between points. If a 1km section has a 20% incline, highlight that segment in **Red** on the map to warn the user.  
* **Estimated Time of Arrival (ETA):** Use "Naismith’s Rule" (a formula for hiking speed based on incline) to give users a highly accurate ETA based on the specific elevation profile of your curated GPX.

### **4\. The "Social" Capacity: Community Extensions**

GPX is extensible. You can add "Custom Extensions" (tags like `<app:rating>` or `<app:photo_spot>`) to the XML.

* **The "Photo Op" Score:** Tag specific points on the route as "High Contrast" or "Sunset Spot." Your app can then notify users: *"In 500m, you'll hit the best view of the valley. Get your camera ready."*  
* **Crowdsourced Detours:** Allow users to "fork" your curated GPX. If a traveler finds a better path around a mudslide, they can upload their version, and your app can show "Community Variations" of your curated expedition.

### **5\. Technical Strategy: "Snap-to-Road" vs. Raw GPX**

Raw GPX data can be "jagged" because GPS isn't 100% accurate.

* **The "Professional" Look:** Use an API like **Mapbox Directions** or **OSRM** to "Snap" your GPX points to the actual roads or trails. This creates a clean, smooth line that looks professional rather than a series of zig-zags.  
* **Offline Mode:** GPX files are tiny (usually \< 1MB). Your app should allow users to download the GPX \+ a small "buffer" of map data for offline use. This is non-negotiable for "Rainforest" or "Desert" expeditions where there is no signal.

### **Summary of GPX Attributes you should utilize:**

| Tag | Use Case in Your App |
| ----- | ----- |
| **`<ele>`** | Create elevation charts and "calories burned" estimates. |
| **`<time>`** | Analyze pace or show "Best time of day" for a specific view. |
| **`<cmt>` / `<desc>`** | Store the "Curated" story/facts about a specific shop or movie site. |
| **`<link>`** | Link to high-res images, menu PDFs, or booking sites for a stop. |
| **`<magvar>`** | (Magnetic Variation) Useful for high-end compass navigation features on trail routes. |
